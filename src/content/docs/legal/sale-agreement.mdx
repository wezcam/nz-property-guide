Now that your property is on the market, an interested buyer will eventually ask to make an offer. Because you followed the setup steps, you won't be scrambling—you will simply point them to the **Pre-Prepared Contract Pack** your solicitor set up for you.

When the buyer's lawyer fills out that ADLS/REINZ agreement and hands it back to you, it becomes a formal, written offer. Here is exactly how to manage that contract safely without an agent mediating.

---

## The Golden Rule: Never Sign on the Spot

<Aside type="danger" title="Absolute Contract Rule">
  Never sign a Sale and Purchase Agreement—or accept a written offer—the moment it is handed to you. A verbal agreement means nothing in NZ property law; only the written signature counts. Once both you and the buyer sign that document, it becomes a legally binding contract. Always tell the buyer: *"Thank you, I am forwarding this straight to my solicitor for a quick review before signing."*
</Aside>

---

## The Offer Management Workflow

Without an agent acting as the middleman, the negotiation process follows a clean, direct line between your lawyer and theirs:

1. **The Buyer Drafts the Offer:** The buyer takes your Pre-Prepared Pack (which already includes your title and land disclosures) and passes it to their lawyer. Their lawyer fills in the purchase price they want to pay, the deposit size, and the timeframes for their conditions.
2. **The Buyer Signs & Delivers:** The buyer signs the agreement first, making it a formal offer, and emails or hands it to you.
3. **The Legal Review:** You immediately forward the PDF straight to your conveyancing solicitor. Your lawyer will check their conditions and make sure they haven't crossed out your custom disclosure clauses.
4. **Counter-Offers (If Needed):** If the price is too low, you don't reject it outright. Your lawyer will cross out their number, write your counter-price above it, initial the change, and send it back. This back-and-forth continues until both parties initial all changes.

## Key Sections of the Offer to Verify

When reviewing the incoming document with your lawyer, focus heavily on these four areas:

### 1. The Purchase Price & Deposit
*   **The Price:** Ensure the value is written clearly in both text and figures.
*   **The Deposit:** This is typically **10% of the purchase price**. As a private seller, ensure the contract states this deposit **must be paid into your solicitor’s trust account** immediately upon the contract being signed or going conditional. *Never accept a deposit directly into your personal bank account.*

### 2. Standard Buyer Conditions
Most Kiwi buyers need time to secure final approvals before the sale is locked in. The standard timeframes you will see written into the front page include:

| Condition | Standard NZ Timeframe | What You Are Agreeing To |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Finance** | 5 to 10 working days | The buyer’s bank must formally approve their mortgage for your specific property address. |
| **Building Report** | 10 working days | A registered building inspector checks the home for structural integrity, moisture levels, or roofing defects. |
| **LIM Report** | 10 to 15 working days | The buyer checks a Land Information Memorandum from the council for historic resource consents or flood plain hazards. |

### 3. Special Clauses & The "Escape Clause"
Look closely at the back page under **"Further Terms of Sale"**. Buyers may try to add custom text, most notably a *"Subject to Sale"* clause (meaning they will only buy your house if they successfully sell their own home first). 

<Aside type="caution" title="Protecting Your Timeline">
  If you accept a "Subject to Sale" offer, your lawyer must insert a **"Cash-Out / Escape Clause"** (usually 3 to 5 working days). This allows you to keep marketing your home to other buyers. If you receive a better cash offer later, you can give the first buyer 3-5 days to go unconditional, or you can legally cancel their contract and take the new cash offer.
</Aside>
