The biggest mistake private sellers make is pricing their home based on emotion, what they paid for it years ago, or what they *need* to unlock their next house. Buyers don't care about any of those things—they only care about current market reality.

Because you aren't paying a traditional real estate agent a massive commission, you have a massive competitive advantage. You can price your home sharply and cleanly. Here is the exact framework to find your magic number.

---

## Step 1: Gather Your Free Digital Data

Before paying for professional valuations, pull the public data that Kiwi buyers will be looking at. In New Zealand, three core platforms drive consumer expectations:

1. **Homes.co.nz:** Look up your address to see your "HomesEstimate." Note the estimated value range (e.g., \$820k – \$890k). 
2. **OneRoof.co.nz:** Check their automated valuation model (AVM). It often uses different data algorithms than Homes.co.nz.
3. **QV.co.nz (Quotable Value):** Look up your property's latest Capital Value (RV/CV).

<Aside type="caution" title="Important About CV/RV">
  Your local Council Rating Value (CV or RV) is built entirely for municipal **tax rating purposes**, not live market value. If your council did valuations during the peak of the market, your CV might be higher than what the house is worth today. If they did it years ago, it will be too low. Never rely solely on CV.
</Aside>

---

## Step 2: Build a CMA (Comparative Market Analysis)

Buyers judge the value of your home by comparing it to houses they walked through last weekend. You need to do the exact same thing. 

Go to the "Sold" tabs on Trade Me Property, OneRoof, and Realestate.co.nz. Find **3 to 5 properties** that meet these criteria:
*   Located within your immediate suburb (ideally within a 1–2km radius).
*   Sold within the **last 3 to 6 months** (do not look at older data, as the NZ market shifts quickly).
*   Similar layout (e.g., 3 bedrooms, 2 bathrooms, standalone brick and tile).

Create a simple spreadsheet comparing their land size, floor area, overall condition, and final sale price against your own home.

## Step 3: Face the Real Numbers (The Math Breakdown)

Let's look at a realistic financial comparison for a **\$1,000,000 NZD** home sale. Real estate agents typically charge a tiered commission (e.g., 3% on the first \$400k, 2% on the remainder plus GST) alongside substantial corporate marketing markups. 

As a private seller, you pay **\$0** in commission. While you choose to pay for high-quality professional photography and a proactive council LIM report out of pocket to completely de-risk the process for buyers, your net savings remain massive.

| **Target Market Sale Price** | \$1,000,000 | \$1,000,000 |

**The Strategy:** Even after paying out of pocket for premium marketing, beautiful professional photos, and a comprehensive council history document, you walk away with an extra **\$27,301** in clear profit. This gives you incredible financial leverage when negotiating with serious buyers.

## Step 4: Choose Your Pricing Strategy

When you list on Trade Me, you must choose how to market the price. In New Zealand, four methods work best for private sellers:

### 1. Deadline Sale ("Unless Sold Prior")
Market the home with a set closing date (e.g., 3 weeks out) but **no advertised price**. Buyers must submit written offers on a Sale & Purchase Agreement by that date. 
* **Why it works:** It forces a sense of urgency on buyers without the stress of an auction room. Crucially, the "Unless Sold Prior" tag means if a buyer brings you a fantastic offer on day 4, you can legally accept it on the spot and end the campaign early.

### 2. "Enquiries Over \$X"
Set the baseline price roughly 5% below your target number. If you want \$850,000, list it as *"Enquiries Over \$809,000"*. This pulls your house into lower search filter brackets, driving massive foot traffic to your first weekend open homes.

### 3. Fixed BEO (Buyer Enquiry Over)
A firm, clear baseline price. Tell the market exactly what the minimum acceptable starting point is to screen out bargain hunters early.

### 4. By Negotiation
Best if your property is truly unique or luxury, making it hard to find local comparable sales data. 

<Aside type="danger" title="Avoid Tenders or Auctions">
  As a private seller, avoid running a standard Auction or a formal sealed Tender process. Buyers are already slightly hesitant about private sales; adding the intense, high-stress legal constraints of an auction room without an agent acting as a buffer usually scares them away. Stick to a Deadline Sale if you want urgency.
</Aside>
